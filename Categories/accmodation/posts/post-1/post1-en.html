Introduction

We understand that as a newcomer in Canada, one of the primary concerns is finding suitable accommodation. The process of renting a place can be daunting, especially given the high demand for rental properties and the rising prices in Toronto because of the increasing number of immigrants each year.

We believe that many new newcomers are facing similar challenges: where should they live? What housing options are available? How to rent an apartment? How much does it typically cost?
If you are in a situation like ours, with limited funds, no car, and no property, you may wonder what choices you have.

Therefore, we would like to share our experiences and provide an overview of the common housing options in Toronto, compare the costs associated with each option, and highlight popular areas for newcomers to consider.

Lastly, We will provide a step-by-step guide on renting a condominium, as it is a popular and favorable choice for many newcomers. We hope this information will serve as a helpful resource for everyone.

Rent BEFORE/ AFTER arriving in Canada?

Actually, there are pros and cons to both renting before arrival and renting after arrival. Both options have their merits. In our case, we chose to rent after arriving in Canada. Firstly, we felt that seeing the property in person would be more reliable. Secondly, having face-to-face interactions would be more convenient than relying on online virtual tours or finding an agent to assist us remotely. Lastly, we were in a bit of a rush and didn't have much time to prepare or view properties while still in Hong Kong. Hence, we ultimately decided to rent a place after arriving in Canada.


For the first month after our arrival, we opted to rent an Airbnb. Airbnb rentals usually provide furniture and utilities, allowing us to focus on other important tasks such as getting a phone plan, setting up a bank account, obtaining a SIN number, and getting a Presto card, among other things. Renting an Airbnb allowed us to settle down without the hassle of purchasing furniture right away. Additionally, having a month's time was sufficient to search for and rent a more permanent condo, so we chose to stay in an Airbnb for the first month and then find a condo to rent.

üí°Tips:
  • Renting an Airbnb for A Month or longer is tax-exempt.
    (This means that the cost of renting for 20 days with taxes included might be similar to renting for a full month without taxes. Therefore, renting for a month can offer better value for money)
  • It is advisable to rent an Airbnb until the END or BEGINNING of a Month since many rental properties require tenants to move in on the 1st of the Month.

Popular Areas for Residence among HK People

  • North york
  • Downtown
  • Scarbough
  • Vaughan
  • Etiobicoke

In the end, we decided to rent a place in Downtown. Since we didn't have a car, our only option was living in Downtown. We wanted to be able to walk to school and supermarkets as much as possible. Commuting can be time-consuming, especially during winter, and the transportation costs can add up significantly each month. Also, it can be quite challenging to walk long distances in the cold winter weather when going grocery shopping. Hence, downtown would be the best choice for us right now.

There are three main drawbacks of living in Downtown. Firstly, there is a lot of noise due to ongoing construction activities, increased foot traffic, and busy transportation routes. Secondly, there are many wanderers and people with mental illnesses on the streets. Lastly, the overall cleanliness may be lower compared to other areas. However, we still find these factors acceptable. Downtown is generally safe during the daytime. The majority of individuals with mental illnesses are unlikely to pose a threat, and many districts in Downtown are quite friendly to live as well, such as Harbourfront. Therefore, we believe DT is still worth considering as a place to live.

Step-by-step Guide - How to Rent a Condo:

A. What to consider before renting?

  • Location - Transportation & Groceries
  • Parking
  • Basic appliances: dishwasher, oven, microwave, fridge, laundry suite(wash and dry)
  • Budget
    • Rent
      ‚Üí For student: usually required at least 1 year tenancy period
      ‚Üí 1-year rent deposit: After first year, rent is submitted every month instead
    • Utility fee
      • Water ~$60-70/mont
      • Heating ~$50-60/month
      • Hydro ~$70-100/month
    • Tenant insurance
      • $30-40/month
    • Internet
      • $60-80/month
    • Key deposit
      • $300-$500 (one time)
  • Amenities (gym room, swimming pool etc.)
  • Pet/ Smoking allowed?
  • Age of Building
    • too new: amenities and some appliances may not be available, construction noise
    • too old: many dusts, some appliances may require maintenance

B. What to prepare before renting?

  • local phone number
  • local bank account
  • Sufficient amount for 1 year of rent
  • passport
  • Study/work permits
  • Letter of school acceptance
  • Financial proof
